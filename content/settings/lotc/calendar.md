Tsurukokans live their lives by the calendar. The calendar tells them when it is time to plant rice, time to harvest, time to prepare for winter, time to worship their ancestors, and time to celebrate. The calendar tells them which days are auspicious, and which days are prone to misfortune.
The Tsurukokan calendar is based upon lunar observation, and has 12 months of 30 days.

Each month contains five weeks, and each week contains six days. Each day has certain superstitions associated with it, and typical Tsurukokans plan their activities around these superstitions.

| Day      | Kanji | Superstition                                         |
| :------- | :---- | :--------------------------------------------------- |
| Senshō  | 先勝  | Good luck in the morning, bad luck in the afternoon. |
| Tomobiki | 友引  | Good luck, except at noon.                           |
| Senbu    | 先負  | Bad luck in the morning, good luck in the afternoon. |
| Metsu    | 滅    | Bad luck all day.                                    |
| Taian    | 大安  | Good luck all day.                                   |
| Shakkō  | 赤口  | Bad luck, except at noon.                            |

Taian is usually the day when ceremonies occur, new journeys are started, and projects begin. Metsu is the day when most Tsurukokans rest, trying to avoid as much activity as possible to lessen the possibility of encountering bad luck.

Additionally, each year is associated with an animal. There are twelve animals, and each is supposed to impart characteristics to those born during that year. However, there are also 5 elements, and each year is fully described by the combination of and element and an animal.

**Elements**
1. Earth
2. Metal
3. Fire
4. Water
5. Wood

**Animals**
1. Rat
2. Ox
3. Tiger
4. Hare
5. Dragon
6. Snake
7. Horse
8. Ram
9. Monkey
10. Crane
11. Dog
12. Boar

Five of these years hold special significance in Tsurukokan culture. The years of the Metal Horse, Wood Ram, Water Dragon, Earth Tiger, and Fire Crane correspond to the five spiritual guardians of Tsurukoku. In each of these years, the major clans send offerings to the far corners of the Empire to secure the guardians' favor.

### Holidays

Tsurukokans celebrate a number of holidays. Some holidays are intended to venerate spirits, some to honor ancestors, and some to mark the changing of the seasons. Among the most important of holidays is the Obon festival, held in Hatsuki, and a group of five holidays known as the sekku, or seasonal festivals. These are:

* 1/7 – Nanakusa no sekku (七草の節句) – during the Festival of Seven Herbs, Tsurukokans eat a rice porridge made with the seven herbs of spring: water dropwort, shepherd’s purse, cudweed, chickweed, nipplewort, turnip, and daikon. The ceremony is intended to usher in the New Year and to ensure longevity.
* 3/3 – Hinamatsuri (雛祭) is the Tsurukokan Doll Festival. On this day, platforms of red cloth are used to display porcelain dolls representing the Emperor, Empress, and members of the court. It is thought that the dolls will chase bad spirits away, and thus protect the family for another year. The dolls must be put away before midnight, though, or misfortune will follow.
* 5/5 – Tango no Sekku (端午の節句) is the Festival of Banners. On this day, Tsurukokans celebrate the arrival of summer rainy season by flying carp-shaped flags and kites – one for each member of the family. It is believed that the rain will purify the rice fields and wash away any evil spirits who might seek to harm the crop.
* 7/7 – Tanabata (七夕) – The Festival of Stars celebrates the love between Orihime, the daughter of Lady Matataki, spirit of the stars, and Hikoboshi, a lowly cowherd who ascended to heaven. The two lovers are separated by a river of stars that crosses the sky, and it is only on this day, each year, that a bridge forms, allowing them to meet.
* 9/9 – Chōyō (重陽) – on Chrysanthemum Day, the beauty of the chrysanthemum flower is celebrated. Houses are filled with flowers, chrysanthemum wine and cakes are consumed, and the beauty of nature is revered.

Other holidays include:

* 2/3 – Setsubun (節分) marks the beginning of spring. During the day, soybeans are thrown from the windows and doorways of houses, while chanting “Out with the oni, in with good fortune.” Each person then eats one soybean for each year of age.
* 4/8 – Hana Matsuri (花祭) marks the birthday of Daruma, the founder of Darumado. Throughout the Empire, people bring offerings of sake and amacha (hydrangea tea) to Darumist temples.
* 6/28 – Tennō tanjōbi (天皇誕生日) is the Emperor’s Birthday. Feasts are held in the Emperor’s honor, and ceremonies are performed at temples and shrines to ensure his long life and a productive reign. On this day, peasants are allowed inside the imperial palace, where the Emperor blesses them and wishes for them a long life.
* 8/13 – 8/15 – *Obon* (お盆, O-bon) is a Darumist holiday dedicated to alleviating the suffering of one’s ancestors. A great feast is held on the first night in order to lure the sprits of the deceased out of Yomi. On the second night, a great dance is held to celebrate the spirits’ temporary freedom. On the third night, paper lanterns are floated down rivers and streams, to guide the spirits of the dead back to Yomi.
* 11/15 – On Shichigosan (七五三), parents celebrate children who have reached the age of three, five, or seven. The children are taken to local Shintō shrines, where they participate in purification rituals and are given “thousand-year candy” to ensure a long life.
* 12/30 – Ōmisoka (大晦日) is the last day of the year. On this day, Tsurukokans clean their houses, their businesses, and their lives. They change the paper on sliding doors and set tatami out in the sun to dry. In the evening, they gather together, eat noodles, and drink sake. At midnight, Darumist temples ring their temple bells 108 times, while people from all over the Land of the Crane celebrate the New Year.

Kaminatsuki is the “Month of no Gods.” During Kaminatsuki, all of the kami gather in Takama no Hara at the behest of the Jade Emperor for a yearly meeting where they discuss how they will run the cosmos in the upcoming year. Thus, there are no holidays during this month.
