You are alone.

You’re in the middle of the woods, having not seen another human in at least eight hours, and it’s *absolutely glorious.*

> Who are you? Record your name on your character sheet. If this is the first time you’ve played this game, your level is one, and you can color in one of your dice.

Disconnecting has always been your go-to coping mechanism. Some people drink and party, some lie on a sunny beach — and while both of those are just fine, thank you, they don’t recharge you. They don't relax you. To do that, you need to get away. Away from life, away from work, away from people, away from everything.

> {{< fa fa-terminal >}} What is your life like? What do you do for work? Who are the people you occasionally need to get away from?

So now you’re in the middle of the woods in early autumn, having pitched your tent next to a small pond, miles down an unmarked, barely discernible trail, getting good and proper *away* from it all. You left your cell phone in the car like you always do when you come out here, because what's the point of disconnecting if you're still connected? Calls and messages and emails and chats are part of the problem.

No, what you need is the sound of birds singing and frogs croaking and twigs crunching under your feet. You need the feel of the breeze as it rustles through the leaves and the smell of campfire smoke. You need mushrooms, and moss, and squirrels, and chipmunks, and small, non-threatening snakes, and crickets, and fallen logs, and tree stumps that look like fairy houses.

That’s what you need. What you do not need is anyone calling or messaging or emailing you right now, thank you very much.

> {{< fa fa-terminal >}} What is the primary source of stress in your life right now? How do you manifest that stress? Do you feel short of breath or experience pounding in your chest?

Ugh, the thought of that makes you want to hyperventilate. What a cluster! That’s why you’re in the middle of the woods right now, miles down a trail you’ve never followed before and which you’re not absolutely certain is actually a trail, miles away from all of that — so you can finally breathe.

Out here, the breathing is good. You’ve spent the afternoon tending to your campsite, chopping firewood, cooking dinner, and soaking in the sounds and sights of the forest.

As the last rays of the sun filter through the tops of the trees, you settle in, tend to your fire, and relax some more. Occasionally you get up to put on another log, pee in the woods, or clean up your campsite from the day’s activities.

> {{< fa fa-terminal >}} Choose one item of camping gear to stow in your backpack. Check it off on your character sheet.

A primary benefit of camping underneath the stars is going to bed when the sun sets. With no screens to distract you, no lights (except your campfire) to interfere with your circadian rhythm, no laundry or vacuuming or dishes to clean, you can fall asleep as soon as you’re tired, just as nature intended.

After a few yawns, you pack it in for the night. Your fire has burned down to a few glowing coals and the autumn night air is growing cool, so you climb into your tent, slip into your sleeping bag, and immediately fall deeply, gloriously asleep.

> {{< fa fa-terminal >}} You have a nightmare. It's the one that you always have when you're feeling too stressed out. What is the object of your fear in this nightmare? How does it play out?

This particular nightmare wakes you up with a start every single time you have it, and this time is no exception. You sit bolt upright in your tent and, after your heart stops pounding, you realize that something is wrong. When you went to sleep, you heard the normal sounds of an autumn evening in the deep woods: crickets singing the song of summer’s end, the chatter of squirrels gathering acorns, owls waking up and other birds nesting for the night.

But now the forest is completely silent. You hear the gentle rustling of leaves in the evening breeze, but that’s it. No chirping, hooting, or chittering. Nothing.

And that’s when you notice the stench. It smells like a dead raccoon was left in a full trashcan in the summer sun for a month. It’s immediately nauseating and even causes you to retch. You unzip the tent flaps and peer out into the darkness. Your campfire is completely out. The only light comes from the stars and the half-moon, which is currently hiding behind the clouds.

Then, despite the nauseating stench, your stomach growls — a deep, empty growl that elicits a hunger that you, a gainfully employed citizen of the first world, have never experienced. Between the disorientation caused by the silence, the nausea, and the hunger, you feel like the world is turning upside down and you’re barely holding on.

You step out of your tent to investigate further, holding onto the frame to steady yourself, and the clouds part. There, standing about a hundred feet away, silhouetted against the silvery light of the night sky, is a man. You can’t see what, if anything, he’s wearing, but he is exceptionally tall and painfully thin — except for a massive, distended belly. To make matters stranger, growing out of the top of his head are a set of deer antlers.

You can feel him looking right at you — maybe even right into you. You realize that the smell is coming from him — but not only the smell. So is the hunger.

You were disoriented, confused, half-asleep. You are now wide awake. You know that if you do not flee immediately, you will never leave the woods alive. Nobody will ever see you again. Nobody will ever find your body.

You grab your backpack and begin to run.
