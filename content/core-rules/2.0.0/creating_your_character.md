To create your character, you'll start with basic characteristics like your name, appearance, and personality. Heroic Tales uses an optional framework called OCEAN to help create a personality, but your character’s thoughts and actions are always entirely yours to determine.

Next, you'll assign dice to the three `traits` every character posses: `body`, `mind`, and `spirit`. You’ll also choose a beginning `talent` — a special ability that can help you overcome challenges.

Record all of these items on your character sheet or somewhere else you can easily access.

- **Name** — your name should be appropriate to the setting. If you’re struggling for ideas, try a random name generator or ask your group for help.

- **Appearance** — describe what your character looks like. How do they dress? What equipment are they carrying? Do they have any specific physical mannerisms that others would notice?

## Personality

Think a little bit about what motivates your character. Why are you a hero? What makes you sad? What are your hopes and fears? How do you interact with other people? With robots? With goblins? With super spies or nefarious villains?

The OCEAN model contains five broad traits that describe the personality and temperament of sentient biological and built creatures. Think about the answers to the preceding questions, and then, for each trait, choose whether your character exhibits low, medium, or high levels of that trait. This will give you a hint as to how your character will usually react in normal situations.

Heroes routinely get themselves into abnormal situations, however. Remember that the OCEAN model is just a useful tool — your character’s thoughts and actions are always yours to control.

**Openness** describes an appreciation for adventure, creativity, and emotion.

- Low — close-minded, literal, enjoys having a routine
- Medium — enjoys new experiences but seeks comfort in the familiar
- High — imaginative, curious, and open-minded

**Conscientiousness** describes the tendency to display self-discipline and act dutifully.

- Low — spontaneous but also sloppy and irresponsible
- Medium — flexible but capable of organization
- High — self-disciplined, prefers to follow a plan

**Extraversion** is characterized by a pronounced interaction with the external world.

- Low — quiet, solitary, and reserved; recharges by being alone
- Medium — able to listen but also be assertive; balanced energy
- High — assertive, high energy, and outgoing; recharges by interacting with others

**Agreeableness** reflects a general concern for social harmony.

- Low — manipulative, callous, aggressive, competitive
- Medium — plays well with others, but capable of irritation
- High — cooperative, polite, kind, and friendly

**Neuroticism** is the tendency to experience negative emotions like anger or anxiety.

- Low — emotionally stable, calm under pressure
- Medium — usually in control of emotions, can break down under severe pressure
- High — low tolerance for stress, emotionally reactive

## Traits

As a hero, you possess a hodgepodge of skills and knowledge. In most any situation you face, you can figure out *what* to do to survive, Therefore, instead of a list of specific skills, every character in Heroic Tales has three traits that reflect general approaches to tackling a challenge: body, mind, and spirit.

- **Body** — use your Body trait when you primarily rely on your physical skills and abilities to overcome a challenge. Fighting hostile aliens or climbing the outside of a mystical temple to the gods use your Body trait.

- **Mind** — use your Mind trait when you primarily rely on remembering information, solving puzzles, or using facts and logic to persuade someone to do something. If you need to figure out how to hot-wire a derelict freighter or decipher a draconic love note, you’ll use your Mind trait.

- **Spirit** — use your Spirit trait when you primarily rely on your force of will or social skills to face down an obstacle. If you're trying to charm a gnomish merchant into selling you their last healing potion or withstand the painful psychic blast of a mentalist supervillain, you’ll use your Spirit trait.

When you create your character, distribute six dice among the three traits. You can have a character with two dice in each trait; or one with three dice in one trait, two dice in another, and one die in the last. This distribution of dice will remain static throughout your character’s adventuring career.

During each `round` of a `challenge`, you choose one of the three traits as your primary trait to reflect your character's actions. Sometimes, more than one trait might appear to apply. In that case, you'll use whichever trait is the strongest.

## Talents

In addition to `traits`, every character starts with a special ability, called a `talent`, that can be used once per challenge. Talents take a form that is appropriate to the game setting and grant one of the following benefits, chosen when you take the talent:

1. Grant yourself `advantage` on your next roll.
2. Convert one `failure` to one `success` on your next roll.
3. Re-roll any `neutral` results on one roll.
4. Grant each of your teammates the ability to ignore one failure on their next roll.
5. Grant each of your teammates advantage on their next roll in exchange for taking `disadvantage` on your next roll.
6. Grant one of your teammates the ability to use one of your traits as part of their action.

## Resources

Heroic Tales uses a generalized form of currency referred to as `credits (¢)`. In campaigns where trade is common, everyone has some amount of access to credits.

A credit can be divided into smaller units, known as `millicredits (m¢)`. One thousand millicredits make up a credit. Similarly, expensive goods are often bought and sold using `kilocredits (k¢)`, which consist of one thousand credits.

## Advancement

Over time, a team will become better at what they do. Individual characters will learn and grow, but the synergy between team members will also improve. Because teams often learn more by failing than by completing a challenge successfully, each time the team fails a challenge, they earn one `experience point`. When a team earns five experience points, their `level` increases by one.

> Milestone advancement: instead of accumulating experience points, a GM may elect to increase a team’s level when they achieve certain goals. During a single campaign, the GM may elect to use whatever method is most suitable to the story, as long as they communicate the decision clearly to the players.

The team’s level starts at one and goes to a maximum of ten. Each character’s `base dice` equals half the team’s level (rounded up), which means that it increases by one at every odd level. At each even level, each player may choose an additional `talent` to add to their character.
