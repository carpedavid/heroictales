Getting Started

October 23, 2020

Heroic Tales is designed for a group of one to six players plus one GM. More than six players is a big group and, especially with the collaborative nature of storytelling in this system, can result in people feeling left out or unheard.

One person in the group needs to assume the role of Game Master (GM). You, lucky person, will provide a loose plot (since no plot survives contact with the players unscathed), set the scene, and narrate the outcomes of dice rolls for the rest of the people in your group. You are responsible for playing all of the characters, creatures, and intelligent super-storms that the player characters will encounter during the story.

Everyone else assumes the role of a player and will take on the persona of a character for the duration of the game. As a team, the players tackle challenges, the meat of the Heroic Tales system. Challenges can be anything from combat to exploration to social interaction; they represent the obstacles that the team is trying to overcome.

A series of challenges linked by a plot is known as an adventure. An adventure is like a short story or chapter of a novel. It is largely self-contained, but often ties into a longer, overarching narrative.

When a set of characters embarks on a series of adventures together, it is known as a campaign. Over the course of a campaign, your characters will experience highs and lows; succeed heroically and fail dramatically; and grow both individually and as a team.

Session Zero

To start a Heroic Tales game, you must first determine a premise for the campaign. As a group, you should discuss what sort of story you want to tell and what sort of characters you want to play. Both players and GM should agree on a concept so that the experience will be entertaining and enriching for all. For example, you might decide to play a class of student superheroes in Super City; a band of zombie-plague survivors barely hanging onto sanity in Run for Your Life; a crew of jazz-loving space explorers in Four Ton Mantis; or a company of adventurers exploring a ruined world in Power & Glory.

As part of this discussion, you should also transparently discuss what topics or content is off limits. The tone and feel of the story may strongly imply certain boundaries, but some players simply don’t feel comfortable exploring certain topics as part of a game. Agreeing on these limits in advance is important.

Creating a Team

Once the campaign concept has been determined, it is time to create your team. One player should record the following information for the group:

  • Name — maybe you’re a group of adventurers known as the Perilous Peregrine Questing Company or a guild of ninja known as the Azure Hand. Perhaps you’re a team of superheroes known as the Wildcats or part of a group of villains that the media has dubbed the Terrible Ten. Even if you don’t use it in character, give yourselves a team name. It’s more fun that way.
  • Origin story — why is your team a team? How did you all come to meet and how did you figure out how to effectively work together?
  • Power level — every team starts at a power level of 1. This will increase over time as your team becomes more effective at working together.
  • Momentum — this numeric value represents your team’s ability to overcome adversity. You will begin each challenge with an amount of momentum based on your team size. Depending on your dice rolls, your momentum may decrease during play, but it will never increase above this value.

Creating Your Character

Based on the team concept, each player will create the details of their team member. Start with basic characteristics like your character’s name, appearance, and personality. Then, choose a set of traits for your character that reflect the things your character knows and the things that your character can do.

  • Name — your name should be appropriate to the setting. If you’re struggling for ideas, try a random name generator or ask your group for help.
  • Appearance — what does your character look like? How do they dress? What equipment are they carrying? Do they have any specific physical mannerisms that others would notice?
  • Personality — what motivates you? What makes you sad? What are your hopes and fears? How do you interact with other people? With animals? With intelligent cyclones?

Traits

Your character’s traits factor into play when your team is tackling a challenge. By weaving details of your character’s abilities (things your character can do) and interests (things your character knows) into the description of your actions, you can influence the number of dice you roll when trying to overcome the challenge.

The level of a trait (beginner, intermediate, and advanced) determines how many dice it contributes to your roll. A beginner trait adds one die, intermediate two dice, and advanced three dice. The maximum number of dice that your traits can contribute to a roll is three, regardless of how many you are able to incorporate into your character’s actions.

Your character begins the story with one ability and one interest at each of the three levels (for a total of six traits).

Abilities — a character’s abilities can either be skills they’ve spent time practicing, like calligraphy, rock climbing, alchemy, ballroom dancing, or calculus; or powers that they acquired by other means, like super strength, jujutsu, necromancy, or invisibility.

Interests — a character’s interests include subjects that they know more about than most other people. For example: particle physics, occult languages, the economy of dwarven nations, or xenobiology.

The scope of each trait is largely determined by the setting of the story. For example, in a high fantasy setting, where magic is common, “magic” is probably too general in scope for a trait. By contrast, a space opera might have low amounts of magic, so a trait called “the Force” might be just fine. Work with your GM to determine how to define the scope of traits for your setting.

Boons

In addition to traits, every character starts with a special ability, called a boon, that can be used once per challenge. Boons are narrowly defined abilities that grant one of the following benefits in specific situations:

  1. Grant yourself advantage on your next roll.
  2. Convert one failure to one success on your next roll.
  3. Re-roll any neutral results on one roll.
  4. Grant each of your teammates the ability to ignore one failure on their next roll.
  5. Grant each of your teammates advantage on their next roll in exchange for taking disadvantage on your next roll.
  6. Grant one of your teammates the ability to use one of your traits as part of their action.

For example, a ninja might have the following ninjutsu boon: The Art of Giving Back.

Catching a spear is easy. Catching it without getting hurt is hard. Catching it without getting hurt while also spinning it around and stabbing it back into your attacker is nearly impossible without a whole lot of practice. As it turns out, you really like to practice.

In a challenge involving hand to hand combat, you can choose to change one failure into a success. Once you use this ability, you can’t use it again for the rest of the challenge.
